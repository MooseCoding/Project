George Milton's Blog Post

My life before Lennie

My life was decent before I met him. I was like I am now. I wandered from ranch to ranch the moment I turned old enough. My pa and ma threw me onto the streets in the 20s. It was a marvelous time.

I made enough money for a monthly expense at where I wanted. Bars, resturaunts, hotels, and other fun places. I always had $10 saved for an emergency



My life during Lennie

After meeting Lennie's Aunt Clara when I was between 14-20, I developed a liking to him. I started taking this strong young kid to work with me. His aunt was extermely kind to me for this.

A few years pass and Aunt Clara is in the ground. Knowing that Lennie would probably get himself kill'd, I started over again with him.

We go around the ranches like all the other workers do. I spent my money getting someone to watch him and going off on my own.

Eventually we get to a ranch in Weed. Lennie grabs this girl's dress and she screams that she is being jumped. We barely managed to escape the dogs and the crazy "sheriffs" with guns.

We got out of there and made it to a Murray and Ready's.

Got assigned to a ranch in Soledad. And well you know how things went from there. I was so close to taking myself with the giant, but I couldn't bring myself to it.



My life after Lennie

Me, Candy, and Crooks bought ourselves a purty property. I took care of the labor and the other two took care of the gardening, cooking and cleaning.

Candy died in '39. He was out tilling the garden when he dropped to the ground. I looked in his pocket and there was a patch of fur that looked like it was 20 years old.

Crooks was cooking, about 3 years after Candy's death. I heard a crack and a scream of pain. I dropped the alfalfa and ran towards him. Crooks' back had snapped it looked like. The pan of sausage and oil spilled on his face.

I held his head up as he died.

As the lone survivor of the Soledad Rebels, I had to keep the ranch dream going.

I worked up enough to buy that Soledad ranch. I became the new boss. I hired migrants and gave them a place to make money. Over time, I my health worsend. I walk to the brush where I shot Lennie

I grab my trusty luger and fire a shot up in the sky for Lennie. All of the sudden there he is with a massive rabbit. I slowly approach him and he welcomes me home.


From
George Milton, June 15, 1949
